police this for posting {Have you ever celebrated a "statistically significant" result, only to find out it had absolutely zero real-world impact? 📉 You might be confusing statistical significance with practical significance!
Whenever you are presented with a shiny, low p-value, I want you to ask yourself: "What would a statistician think here?" A trained statistician knows that looking at a p-value is only the beginning of the analysis, not the end.
Here is a quick breakdown to help you develop your statistical mindset and spot the difference between a p-value and an effect size.
🔬 What is a P-Value (Statistical Significance)? Formally, a p-value is the probability of obtaining data as extreme as, or more extreme than, your observed results, assuming that the null hypothesis is exactly true (CrossFit, 2020). Intuitively, it answers the question: "How unusual is this result if there is actually no effect?" (Lee & Tabak, n.d.). It helps establish whether a finding is reliable, but it says nothing about the importance of the result (CrossFit, 2020).
⚠️ The "Large Sample" Trap Here is a common misconception: people often believe that a low p-value means the effect is highly important. ❌ Incorrect! P-values are highly dependent on sample size (Epistemic and pragmatic divergences, n.d.). If you have a massive dataset, virtually any microscopic, non-zero difference will cross the threshold into "statistical significance" (Kulkarni, 2026). For example, a study on a new diet pill might show a "statistically significant" difference, but if that difference only amounts to losing one extra ounce over a month, the result is practically meaningless in the real world (Lee & Tabak, n.d.).
📏 The Solution: Effect Size (Practical Significance) To understand if a result is actually useful, you need to look at the effect size. Practical significance asks, "How large is the observed effect, and does it have real-world utility?" (Epistemic and pragmatic divergences, n.d.). Unlike p-values, effect size measurements (like Cohen's d, Hedges' g, or Odds Ratios) strip out the sample-size component, allowing you to quantify the true magnitude of the difference (Kulkarni, 2026; Pennsylvania State University, n.d.).
💡 The Key Takeaways:

* Statistical Significance (P-Value): Tells you if an effect exists and whether it is likely due to chance (Corvi, 2025).
* Practical Significance (Effect Size): Tells you if that effect actually matters in the real world (Corvi, 2025).

To make truly data-driven decisions that impact your business or research, always report effect sizes alongside your p-values!
